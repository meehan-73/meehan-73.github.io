How I... And You!... Got Here

I figure I should give some insight as to my current background and what I'm trying to do. In what follows, you'll see the route I took to become the person I am today.

Lifelong Love of Mathematics

I have always had a love of mathematics. My parents tell me that as a two-year old, I would often place myself directly in front of the TV at night; the NJ Lottery was on, and I absolutely loved watching all of the numbers flop around. Over the years, math has become more than just a subject to me â€” it is my profession, my passion. Very early on (at two years old, apparently), I decided I wanted to learn as much math as I possibly could. In high school, this amounted to taking both AP Calculus AB/BC exams, as well as AP Statistics, but things got more interesting once I got to college.

In college at The University of Notre Dame, I decided to be one of 10-15 students to major in "Honors" Mathematics; Honors Mathematics was a specialized program where students would receive experience in theoretical (think "proof-based") math classes. Not only was arriving at the right answer important, but also the process at which the right answer was found. This turned math upside down for me - it was suddenly a game, a game with which logic was at the forefront and argumentation was key. After all, what's the purpose of a right answer if you can't convincingly defend it? In 2013, I graduated undergrad with a Bachelors of Science in Mathematics, with a thesis on the combinatorial topic of Ramsey Theory. I figured, "Why quit a good thing?", and so onward I went to graduate school at The Ohio State University.

The idea of picking an area of math to focus on for my PhD was more intimidating than I could have ever imagined. "Lions, and tigers, and bears, oh my?"... more like, "Abstract Algebra, and Geometry, Topology, and Differential Equations, and 20+ other areas, oh my!" With my previous experience in Combinatorics, coupled with the idea that I wanted to go into industry after graduation, I settled on Random Matrix Theory - an area that lies within the intersection of Probability, Statistics, and Combinatorics. My advisor, Dr. Hoi Nguyen, was at the forefront of the field and provided insight that was truly invaluable throughout the entire process. In laymen's terms, my focus was on some universality problems; I enjoyed looking at problems where results were known in specific settings, and extending them to more compicated settings. This would mean the result was universal. I think this is a valuable way of looking at things, and it - universality - appears in life more often than you'd expect. What culminated was a doctorate in mathematics and an appreciation for the immense amount of dedication that the PhD process entailed.

Enter Data Science

Toward the end of my graduate education, I decided I wanted to take up programming to supplement what I was seeing in school. After all, what better reality check is there for dealing with random matrices than actually writing out some code to generate the distributions and check how the eigenvectors are behaving? This started my road to data science. I quickly began to realize just how much I enjoy coding, and soon after I turned to modeling and analyzing datasets. I have, elsewhere on this page, a list of the many courses I've taken online to instill a solid foundation in data science. There is always more to learn, and I'm excited to learn it!
